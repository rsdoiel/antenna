In ‚ÄúGenesis,‚Äù an essay in the 2007 collection The Creationists, EL Doctorow (no relation) describes how the ancient Babylonians were so poleaxed by the strange wonder of the story they made up about the origin of the universe that they assumed that it must be true. They themselves weren‚Äôt nearly imaginative enough to have come up with this super-cool tale, so God must have put it in their minds: https://pluralistic.net/2023/04/29/gedankenexperimentwahn/#high-on-your-own-supply That seems to have been what happened to the Air Force colonel who falsely claimed that a ‚Äúrogue AI-powered drone‚Äù had spontaneously evolved the strategy of killing its operator as a way of clearing the obstacle to its main objective, which was killing the enemy: https://pluralistic.net/2023/06/04/ayyyyyy-eyeeeee/ This never happened. It was ‚Äì in the chagrined colonel‚Äôs words ‚Äì a ‚Äúthought experiment.‚Äù In other words, this guy ‚Äì who is the USAF‚Äôs Chief of AI Test and Operations ‚Äì was so excited about his own made up story that he forgot it wasn‚Äôt true and told a whole conference-room full of people that it had actually happened. Maybe that‚Äôs what happened with the George Carlinbot 3000: the Dudesy dudes fell in love with their own vision for a fully automated luxury Carlinbot and forgot that they had made it up, so they just cheated, assuming they would eventually be able to make a fully operational Battle Carlinbot. That‚Äôs basically the Theranos story: a teenaged ‚Äúentrepreneur‚Äù was convinced that she was just about to produce a seemingly impossible, revolutionary diagnostic machine, so she faked its results, abetted by investors, customers and others who wanted to believe: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theranos The thing about stories of AI miracles is that they are peddled by both AI‚Äôs boosters and its critics. For boosters, the value of these tall tales is obvious: if normies can be convinced that AI is capable of performing miracles, they‚Äôll invest in it. They‚Äôll even integrate it into their product offerings and then quietly hire legions of humans to pick up the botshit it leaves behind. These abettors can be relied upon to keep the defects in these products a secret, because they‚Äôll assume that they‚Äôve committed an operator error. After all, everyone knows that AI can do anything, so if it‚Äôs not performing for them, the problem must exist between the keyboard and the chair. But this would only take AI so far. It‚Äôs one thing to hear implausible stories of AI‚Äôs triumph from the people invested in it ‚Äì but what about when AI‚Äôs critics repeat those stories? If your boss thinks an AI can do your job, and AI critics are all running around with their hair on fire, shouting about the coming AI jobpocalypse, then maybe the AI really can do your job? https://locusmag.com/2020/07/cory-doctorow-full-employment/ There‚Äôs a name for this kind of criticism: ‚Äúcriti-hype,‚Äù coined by Lee Vinsel, who points to many reasons for its persistence, including the fact that it constitutes an ‚Äúacademic business-model‚Äù: https://sts-news.medium.com/youre-doing-it-wrong-notes-on-criticism-and-technology-hype-18b08b4307e5 That‚Äôs four reasons: for AI hype: I. to win investors and customers; II. to cover customers‚Äô and users‚Äô embarrassment when the AI doesn‚Äôt perform; III. AI dreamers so high on their own supply that they can‚Äôt tell truth from fantasy; IV. A business-model for doomsayers who form an unholy alliance with AI companies by parroting their silliest hype in warning form. But there‚Äôs a fifth motivation for criti-hype: to simplify otherwise tedious and complex situations. As Jamie Zawinski writes, this is the motivation behind the obvious lie that the ‚Äúautonomous cars‚Äù on the streets of San Francisco have no driver: https://www.jwz.org/blog/2024/01/driverless-cars-always-have-a-driver/ GM‚Äôs Cruise division was forced to shutter its SF operations after one of its ‚Äúself-driving‚Äù cars dragged an injured pedestrian for 20 feet: https://www.wired.com/story/cruise-robotaxi-self-driving-permit-revoked-california/ One of the widely discussed revelations in the wake of the incident was that Cruise employed 1.5 skilled technical remote overseers for every one of its ‚Äúself-driving‚Äù cars. In other words, they had replaced a single low-waged cab driver with 1.5 higher-paid remote operators. As Zawinski writes, SFPD is well aware that there‚Äôs a human being (or more than one human being) responsible for every one of these cars ‚Äì someone who is formally at fault when the cars injure people or damage property. Nevertheless, SFPD and SFMTA maintain that these cars can‚Äôt be cited for moving violations because ‚Äúno one is driving them.‚Äù But figuring out who which person is responsible for a moving violation is ‚Äúcomplicated and annoying to deal with,‚Äù so the fiction persists. (Zawinski notes that even when these people are held responsible, they‚Äôre a ‚Äúmoral crumple zone‚Äù for the company that decided to enroll whole cities in nonconsensual murderbot experiments.) Automation hype has always involved hidden humans. The most famous of these was the ‚Äúmechanical Turk‚Äù hoax: a supposed chess-playing robot that was just a puppet operated by a concealed human operator wedged awkwardly into its carapace. This pattern repeats itself through the ages. Thomas Jefferson ‚Äúreplaced his slaves‚Äù with dumbwaiters ‚Äì but of course, dumbwaiters don‚Äôt replace slaves, they hide slaves: https://www.stuartmcmillen.com/blog/behind-the-dumbwaiter/ The modern Mechanical Turk ‚Äì a division of Amazon that employs low-waged ‚Äúclickworkers,‚Äù many of them overseas ‚Äì modernizes the dumbwaiter by hiding low-waged workforces behind a veneer of automation. The MTurk is an abstract ‚Äúcloud‚Äù of human intelligence (the tasks MTurks perform are called ‚ÄúHITs,‚Äù which stands for ‚ÄúHuman Intelligence Tasks‚Äù). This is such a truism that techies in India joke that ‚ÄúAI‚Äù stands for ‚Äúabsent Indians.‚Äù Or, to use Jathan Sadowski‚Äôs wonderful term: ‚ÄúPotemkin AI‚Äù: https://reallifemag.com/potemkin-ai/ This Potemkin AI is everywhere you look. When Tesla unveiled its humanoid robot Optimus, they made a big flashy show of it, promising a $20,000 automaton was just on the horizon. They failed to mention that Optimus was just a person in a robot suit: https://www.siliconrepublic.com/machines/elon-musk-tesla-robot-optimus-ai Likewise with the famous demo of a ‚Äúfull self-driving‚Äù Tesla, which turned out to be a canned fake: https://www.reuters.com/technology/tesla-video-promoting-self-driving-was-staged-engineer-testifies-2023-01-17/ The most shocking and terrifying and enraging AI demos keep turning out to be ‚ÄúJust A Guy‚Äù (in Molly White‚Äôs excellent parlance): https://twitter.com/molly0xFFF/status/1751670561606971895 And yet, we keep falling for it.
