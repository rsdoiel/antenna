## Former US Senator Inhofe, defense hawk and climate change skeptic, dies at 89

date: 2024-07-09, from: VOA News USA

OKLAHOMA CITY, oklahoma ‚Äî¬†Former Senator Jim Inhofe, a conservative known for his strong support of defense spending and his denial that human activity is responsible for the bulk of climate change, has died. He was 89.¬†


Inhofe, a powerful fixture in Oklahoma politics for more than six decades, died Tuesday morning after suffering a stroke during the July Fourth holiday, his family said in a statement.¬†


Inhofe, a Republican who underwent quadruple bypass heart surgery in 2013 before being elected to a fourth term, was elected to a fifth Senate term in 2020, before stepping down in early 2023.¬†


'The greatest hoax'


Inhofe frequently criticized the mainstream science that human activity contributed to changes in the Earth's climate, once calling it "the greatest hoax ever perpetrated on the American people."¬†


In February 2015, with temperatures in the nation's capital below freezing, Inhofe brought a snowball on to the Senate floor. He tossed it before claiming that environmentalists focus attention on global warming as it kept getting cold.¬†


As Oklahoma's senior U.S. senator, Inhofe was a staunch supporter of the state's five military installations and a vocal fan of congressional earmarks. The Army veteran and licensed pilot, who would fly himself to and from Washington, secured the federal money to fund local road and bridge projects, and criticized House Republicans who wanted a one-year moratorium on such pet projects in 2010.¬†


"Defeating an earmark doesn't save a nickel," Inhofe told the Oklahoma City Chamber of Commerce that August. "It merely means that within the budget process, it goes right back to the bureaucracy."¬†


He was a strong backer of President Donald Trump, who praised him for his "incredible support of our #MAGA agenda" while endorsing the senator's 2020 reelection bid. During the Trump administration, Inhofe served as chair of the Senate Armed Services Committee following the death of Republican Senator John McCain of Arizona.¬†


Closer to home, Inhofe helped secure millions of dollars to clean up a former mining hub in northeast Oklahoma that spent decades on the Environmental Protection Agency's Superfund list. In a massive buyout program, the federal government purchased homes and businesses within the 104-square-kilometer region of Tar Creek, where children consistently tested for dangerous levels of lead in their blood.¬†


Republican U.S. Representative Frank Lucas, the senior member of the Oklahoma congressional delegation, called Inhofe a true public servant.¬†


"His long career in the United States House and Senate serves as a testament to his strong moral compass and innate desire to better his home state," Lucas said in a statement¬†


In 2021, Inhofe defied some in his party by voting to certify Democrat Joe Biden's victory in the presidential election, saying that to do otherwise would be a violation of his oath of office to support and defend the Constitution. He voted against convicting Trump at both of his impeachment trials.¬†


Worked in business, public service


Born James Mountain Inhofe on Nov. 17, 1934, in Des Moines, Iowa, Inhofe grew up in Tulsa, Oklahoma, and received a bachelor's degree in economics from the University of Tulsa in 1959. He served in the Army between 1956 and 1958 and was a businessman for three decades.¬†


He was elected to the state House in 1966 and two years later to the state Senate, where he remained during unsuccessful runs for governor in 1974 and for the U.S. House in 1976. He then won three terms as Tulsa mayor starting in 1978.¬†


Inhofe went on to win two terms in the U.S. House in the 1980s, before throwing his hat into a bitter U.S. Senate race when longtime Senator David Boren resigned in 1994 to become president of the University of Oklahoma. Inhofe beat then-U.S. Representive Dave McCurdy in a special election to serve the final two years of Boren's term and was reelected five times.¬†


Boren, a Democrat, said he and Inhofe worked together in a bipartisan manner when both were in the state Legislature. He later defeated Inhofe in a race for governor.¬†


"While we ran against each other for governor, we were opponents but never enemies and remained friends," Boren said in a statement. "I hope we can rebuild that spirit in American politics."¬†


Frequent flyer


Inhofe was a commercial-rated pilot and flight instructor with more than 50 years of flying experience.¬†


He made an emergency landing in Claremore in 1999, after his plane lost a propeller, an incident later blamed on an installation error. In 2006, his plane spun out of control upon landing in Tulsa; he and an aide escaped injury, though the plane was badly damaged.¬†


In 2010, Inhofe landed his small plane on a closed runway at a rural South Texas airport while flying himself and others to South Padre Island. Runway workers scrambled, and Inhofe agreed to complete a remedial training program rather than face possible legal action.¬†


He later sponsored legislation that expanded the rights of pilots when dealing with Federal Aviation Administration disciplinary proceedings.¬†


Inhofe is survived by his wife, Kay, three children and several grandchildren. A son, Dr. Perry Dyson Inhofe II, died in November 2013, at the age of 51, when the twin-engine aircraft he was flying crashed a few miles north of Tulsa International Airport.

## Climate Scored Some Quasi-Victories in Europe

<img src="https://heatmap.news/media-library/eyJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJpbWFnZSI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXNzZXRzLnJibC5tcy81MjU1ODc1OC9vcmlnaW4uanBnIiwiZXhwaXJlc19hdCI6MTc2MDM1MTMyN30.6wYy0HzBZpl4xU5RV-TkcvrDoJRHK200P00CKP0RT90/image.jpg?width=1200&height=800&coordinates=0%2C0%2C0%2C0"/><br/><br/><p>While America has been distracted by its suddenly-very-real upcoming election, two other important political stories have been unfolding across the pond. ## 3 Takeaways From Our SunZia Investigation

date: 2024-07-08, from: Heatmap News


<img src="https://heatmap.news/media-library/eyJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJpbWFnZSI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXNzZXRzLnJibC5tcy81MjUwMzEzMy9vcmlnaW4uanBnIiwiZXhwaXJlc19hdCI6MTcyNjg0MDI3NH0.hOWgyJa00K8j_57I3vbJWajbXaM-vmMIdpM5ZrIE574/image.jpg?width=1200&height=800&coordinates=0%2C0%2C0%2C0"/><br/><br/><p class="drop-caps">How hard is it to build big clean-energy infrastructure in America? <a href="https://heatmap.news/economy/sunzia" target="_self"><u>Look at SunZia.</u></a></p>
<p>When completed, the more-than-500-mile power line is meant to ferry electricity from a massive new wind farm in New Mexico to the booming power markets of Arizona and California. When finally built, SunZia will be the largest renewable project in the United States, if not the Western Hemisphere.</p>
<p>But as I detail in <a href="https://heatmap.news/economy/sunzia" target="_self"><u>a recent investigation</u></a> for Heatmap, it has taken too long ‚Äî much too long ‚Äî to build. Nearly two decades have elapsed since a project developer first asked the federal government for permission to build SunZia.</p>
<p>Since it was first proposed, SunZia has endured seemingly endless environmental studies and lawsuits. It has been bought, sold, and bargained over. The end result is that a project first conceived in 2006 ‚Äî which was expected to operate in 2013 ‚Äî is now due to open in 2026. </p>
<p>That is a massive problem, because confronting climate change will require the country to build dozens of new long-distance power lines like SunZia. If the United States wants to meet its Paris Agreement goal by 2050, then it will have to triple the size of its power grid in just 26 years, according to <a href="https://netzeroamerica.princeton.edu/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>Princeton‚Äôs Net Zero America study</u></a>. (That research was led by Jesse Jenkins, who co-hosts Heatmap‚Äôs <a href="https://heatmap.news/podcast/shift-key-robinson-meyer-jesse-jenkins-climate-podcast" target="_self"><u>‚ÄúShift Key‚Äù</u></a> podcast with me.)</p>
<p>The country is not on track to meet that goal. <a href="https://heatmap.news/economy/sunzia" target="_self"><u>My story</u></a> on SunZia set out to determine why. </p>
<p>Here are three major takeaways from my investigation:</p><h2>1. Transmission projects face more obstacles than fossil fuel projects ‚Äî even in the eyes of self-described environmentalists.</h2><p>At a fundamental level, a power line and a natural gas pipeline aren‚Äôt so different: Both move a large amount of energy over a long distance.</p>
<p>Yet it is much easier to build a natural gas pipeline than a transmission line, and they face very different regulatory hurdles in America. When a company proposes a new transmission line, it must get permission from every state whose borders it plans to cross. This can result in an arduous, years-long process of application, study, and approval.</p>
<p>That same obstacle does not hinder gas developers. When a company proposes a new natural gas pipeline, it can get many of its permits handled by a single federal agency, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission. FERC is a one-stop shop for gas pipeline developers, organizing and granting state-level permits through a streamlined process. </p>
<p>(To be sure, natural gas pipelines sometimes need permits from other federal agencies ‚Äî such as the Bureau of Land Management ‚Äî before they can begin construction. But transmission developers need to get<em> </em>permits from those other federal agencies, too.)</p>
<p>But not all of the obstacles are regulatory. Transmission and renewable projects simply <em><em>look </em></em>different than pipelines, which can make environmentalists and the public more skeptical of them. Even though pipelines can leak or spill, they can be buried or built closer to the ground than power lines, and therefore pose less of a visual disturbance to the landscape.</p>
<p>In recent years, much of the controversy around SunZia has focused on the San Pedro Valley, a gorgeous desert landscape northeast of Tucson, Arizona. SunZia must pass through the valley to connect to a power station near Phoenix. </p>
<p>Two Native American tribes ‚Äî the Tohono O'odham Nation and the San Carlos Apache Tribe ‚Äî sued to block SunZia last year. They argue that the valley has cultural value and must be preserved intact and undiminished.</p>
<p>But the valley is already home to a large natural gas pipeline, mostly ‚Äî but not entirely ‚Äî buried underground. (The pipeline is on pylons near Redington, Arizona, where it crosses the San Pedro River.)</p>
<p>In an interview, a leader at the Center for Biological Diversity, an environmentalist group that joined the tribes‚Äô lawsuit, said that SunZia‚Äôs proposed power line is problematic in part because it will be so tall. </p>
<p>‚ÄúThere are no 200-foot large power lines going through the San Pedro Valley,‚Äù Robin Silver, the leader, told me. ‚ÄúThe gas pipeline doesn‚Äôt have 200 foot towers.‚Äù</p>
<p>If environmentalists focus on a project‚Äôs visual prominence, then pipelines will virtually always win out over transmission lines.</p>
<p>A federal judge dismissed the tribes‚Äô lawsuit last month. A representative of the Tohono O'odham Nation did not respond to multiple requests for comment.</p><h2>2. A better relationship between conservationists and clean energy developers is possible.</h2>
<p>In permitting debates, conservationists and clean energy developers can often become enemies. Traditional conservationists seek to slow down the permitting process as much as possible and move a project away from a treasured or sensitive area, while developers and climate hawks want to build clean energy infrastructure quickly and efficiently. </p>
<p>These fights often play out as costly lawsuits over the National Environmental Policy Act, a 1970 law that requires the government to study the environmental impact of every decision that it makes. Advocates and opponents wind up battling in court over whether or not a project‚Äôs environmental impact has been sufficiently studied.</p>
<p>That‚Äôs not what happened with SunZia. Some environmentalists and traditional conservation groups, such as the Audubon Society, now praise SunZia‚Äôs process.</p>
<p>It wasn‚Äôt always that way. During the early 2010s, SunZia‚Äôs proposal to cross the Rio Grande in New Mexico was just as controversial as its San Pedro Valley route. <p>That changed after the Defense Department forced a major rethink of the line in 2018. Soon after that, Pattern Energy, a San Francisco-based energy developer, took over the project.</p>
<p>Pattern took a different approach than its predecessor and partnered with environmental groups to learn how it could build the power line in the least intrusive way. </p>
<p>It conducted original research on how sandhill cranes fly, and ‚Äî based on that research ‚Äî moved the power line to the place where it would interfere with birds the least. <p>Pattern also agreed to illuminate the transmission line with an experimental infrared system to make it more visible to birds.</p>
<p>These changes, which also allowed Pattern to avoid a Defense Department site, were so extensive that it had to apply for a new federal permit. </p>
<p>‚ÄúPattern being a company that was willing to have discussions with us in good faith ‚Äî and that conversation happening before the re-permitting process ‚Äî was, I think, really important,‚Äù Jon Hayes, a wildlife biologist and the executive director of Audubon Southwest, told me.</p><h2>3. <p>This collaborative relationship was possible in part because it was facilitated by Senator Martin Heinrich, a Democrat who represents New Mexico.</p>
<p>Heinrich, a climate hawk and the son of a utility worker, had long championed the SunZia project. <p>Silver, the Center for Biological Diversity leader, told me that Heinrich‚Äôs involvement is the principal reason why SunZia has been praised in New Mexico but criticized in Arizona. </p>
<p>The Grand Canyon State doesn‚Äôt have elected officials who were willing to get involved in SunZia and push for a mutually beneficial solution, he said. (For much of the 2010s, Republicans held both of the state‚Äôs Senate seats.)</p>
<p>But a project‚Äôs ultimate success cannot rest on the quality or curiosity of its senators. Martin Heinrich, as a climate solution, doesn‚Äôt scale, and not every clean energy project will have a federal chaperone.</p>
<p>What‚Äôs more, America‚Äôs existing permitting system ‚Äî which is channeled through its adversarial legal system ‚Äî practically discourages cooperation. It pushes developers and their opponents to pursue aggressive and expensive legal campaigns against each other. These campaigns burn huge amounts of time and millions of dollars in legal fees ‚Äî money that could be spent on decarbonizing the economy.</p>
<p>In order to meet America‚Äôs climate goals, developers must build dozens of projects like SunZia, all around the country, in the years to come. That will not happen under today‚Äôs permitting system. The country needs something better.</p>

## Build Clean Energy on Dirty Land, These Researchers Say

date: 2024-07-08, from: Heatmap News


<img src="https://heatmap.news/media-library/eyJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJpbWFnZSI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXNzZXRzLnJibC5tcy81MjI3OTAzNC9vcmlnaW4uanBnIiwiZXhwaXJlc19hdCI6MTc1NzEzOTQ1OX0.ynUgvQKLP0oGPbFsh5pItNUf0k0Z6ujyYpSdbktBEUc/image.jpg?width=1200&height=800&coordinates=0%2C0%2C0%2C0"/><br/><br/><p class="drop-caps">There are some things money can‚Äôt buy, and it seems a clean power grid is one of them. Despite authorizing billions of dollars to subsidize renewable energy development through the Inflation Reduction Act and the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law, the Biden administration remains off track to reach its target of <a href="https://www.energy.gov/sites/default/files/2023-05/DOE%20-%20100%25%20Clean%20Electricity%20-%20Final.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>100% clean electricity by 2035</u></a>. Even after a banner year in which domestic investment hit <a href="https://about.bnef.com/blog/global-clean-energy-investment-jumps-17-hits-1-8-trillion-in-2023-according-to-bloombergnef-report/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>$303 billion</u></a> and the US added <a href="https://repeatproject.org/docs/Clean_Investment_in_2023_02-21-24.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>32.3 gigawatts of new clean electricity capacity</u></a>, the country is still building renewable energy at only <a href="https://energyinnovation.org/publication/reaching-90-percent-clean-electricity-in-the-us-by-2035/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>half the rate that is needed</u></a>.</p>
<p>Among the barriers holding up clean energy deployment, local opposition looms large. As developers seek out new sites on which to build wind and solar, they are repeatedly <a href="https://insideclimatenews.org/news/23022024/community-opposition-and-grid-challenges-slow-pace-of-renewable-efforts/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>finding themselves at odds</u></a> with neighbors who object to their projects on aesthetic, economic, or political grounds. Whether through <a href="https://scholarship.law.columbia.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1201&context=sabin_climate_change" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>formal laws</u></a> or protracted permitting processes, these objections have begun to have a noticeable effect on the pace of renewable energy adoption. In a <a href="https://emp.lbl.gov/news/larg-e-scale-wind-and-solar" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>recent survey</u></a> by the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, wind and solar developers reported seeing roughly a third of their siting applications canceled over the five years prior, with two of the most common reasons being ‚Äúcommunity opposition‚Äù and ‚Äúlocal ordinances or zoning.‚Äù </p>
<p>But what if the solution to this impasse has been hiding in plain sight ‚Äî or more accurately, behind a chain link fence? </p>
<p>The U.S. has around <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1364032113006199#:~:text=It%20is%20estimated%20that%20about,7.3%20TWh%20from%20landfill%20gas." rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>270 million acres</u></a> of so-called ‚Äúmarginal land,‚Äù a designation that includes retired mines, closed landfills, former industrial facilities, brownfield sites, and depleted or unproductive farmland. That‚Äôs around twice the land area that would be required for a renewables-and-nuclear-only power grid, the most land-intensive net-zero scenario <a href="https://www.nrel.gov/docs/fy22osti/81644.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>modeled by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory</u></a>. These neglected properties are more than just an eyesore for neighbors ‚Äî they also represent wasted prospects for economic development, and in many cases pose a contamination risk to the local environment. To law professors <a href="https://michigan.law.umich.edu/faculty-and-scholarship/our-faculty/alexandra-klass" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>Alexandra Klass</u></a> and <a href="https://iee.psu.edu/people/hannah-wiseman" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>Hannah Wiseman</u></a>, however, they are an opportunity in disguise. </p>
<p>In their <a href="https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4747870" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>new paper</u></a>, forthcoming in the <em><em>Minnesota Law Review</em></em>, Klass and Wiseman (of the University of Michigan and Penn State, respectively) propose directing the bulk of new clean energy development to these marginal lands. It‚Äôs a concept they call ‚Äúrepurposed energy,‚Äù and it offers a way to, in one fell swoop, avert local objections, reclaim unproductive land, and create new opportunities for economically dislocated communities. </p>
<p>It‚Äôs not a new concept ‚Äî since 2008, the Environmental Protection Agency‚Äôs <a href="https://www.epa.gov/re-powering" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>RE-Powering America‚Äôs Land Initiative</u></a> has offered funding to developers looking to build renewable energy on potentially contaminated land. </p>
<p>What the new paper proposes, however, is a greater convergence of public benefits on this specific subset of projects, which Klass views as a down payment on societal acceptance. ‚ÄúIf you can come up with a project that‚Äôs going to have community support, that means you can actually build it,‚Äù she told me. ‚ÄúAnd that‚Äôs worth paying a little extra money up front.‚Äù</p>
<p>Consider some of the most common objections to renewable energy siting: that it <a href="https://whyy.org/articles/offshore-wind-critics-say-farms-will-damage-shore-economies-and-ruin-ocean-views/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>ruins the view</u></a>, <a href="https://www.jsonline.com/story/sports/outdoors/2024/03/02/wisconsin-prairie-chickens-threatened-by-proposed-solar-farm-along-buena-vista-state-wildlife-area/72803144007/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>disrupts habitats</u></a>, or <a href="https://www.reuters.com/world/us/solar-capacity-grows-some-americas-most-productive-farmland-is-risk-2024-04-27/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>occupies valuable farmland</u></a>. Each would seem to carry less weight when applied to, say, an abandoned mine instead of a pristine coastline. Throw in low purchase prices, pre-existing transmission lines at retired coal or gas power plants, and the chance to direct jobs and revenue to low-income communities (where contaminated properties are <a href="https://www.epa.gov/ej-research/epa-research-environmental-justice-and-contaminated-sites#:~:text=Low%2Dincome%2C%20tribal%2C%20indigenous,increased%20exposures%20to%20environmental%20hazards." rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>disproportionately located</u></a>), and you‚Äôve got, in theory, an attractive site for a solar or wind farm. </p>
<p>In spite of these upsides, practical examples of repurposed energy remain few and far between. Only 0.7% of the renewable energy capacity installed in the United States since 1997 has been on reclaimed land, <a href="https://www.epa.gov/re-powering" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><u>according to EPA data</u></a>. That‚Äôs because, faced with the possibility of extravagant cleanup costs and liability for prior contamination, most developers prefer to take their chances with a greenfield. </p>
<p>Klass and Wiseman propose a set of policy changes that could, they hope, spur a renewable energy renaissance on marginal lands. First, there are some existing incentives for repurposed energy they propose expanding. Certain state funding programs ‚Äì like Massachusetts‚Äô SMART Program ‚Äì and streamlined permitting processes ‚Äì like New York‚Äôs Build-Ready Program ‚Äì could offer a template for other states seeking to accelerate redevelopment of their own brownfields. Layering more such benefits on top of federal funding opportunities like the IRA‚Äôs Energy Infrastructure and Reinvestment Program, they contend, could help stimulate broader interest from developers. </p>
<p>Second, they offer a set of new, more ambitious reforms to entice clean energy companies onto marginal lands. Among them: </p>
<ul><li>Giving the Department of Energy direct permitting authority over repurposed energy would require an act of Congress, but could accelerate approvals. Klass and Wiseman point out that many sites containing hazardous chemicals may already be in the federal regulatory sphere.</li><li>The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission could direct regional transmission organizations to grant repurposed energy projects priority in interconnection queues, bypassing a lengthy part of the development timeline.</li><li>State offices that oversee brownfield cleanup and development could establish separate divisions for repurposed energy, better equipping them to deal with and resolve clean energy developers‚Äô unique needs. </li></ul>
<p>Klass sees the paper as a timely contribution at a critical juncture for the renewable energy industry. ‚ÄúWe‚Äôre at an important moment in time where there‚Äôs a lot of federal funding available,‚Äù she told me. ‚ÄúBut we are not on track to build the amount of clean energy we need to meet our targets.‚Äù By focusing support on repurposed energy, she thinks policymakers may be able to erode some of the sociopolitical barriers holding back the industry. </p>
<p>There is evidence to support this belief. <p>It‚Äôs a view that resonates in the industry, although that doesn‚Äôt make this kind of development easy. <p>Dedicated state support in places like Massachusetts, Illinois, and Maryland has helped Ameresco alleviate some of the costs. ‚ÄúUtility programs or state administered programs do incentivize the use of these types of projects,‚Äù Mancini said. <p>Michael Gerrard, the founder of Columbia University‚Äôs Sabin Center for Climate Change Law and one of the country‚Äôs foremost environmental lawyers, thinks the idea could accelerate clean energy deployment. ‚ÄúLocal opposition is one of the most important impediments [to renewable energy],‚Äù he pointed out to me. ## Epistemic calibration and searching the space of truth

date: 2024-07-08, from: The Sephist blog

<p>I&rsquo;ve long been enamored by DALL-E 2‚Äôs specific flavor of visual creativity. Especially given the text-to-image AI system‚Äôs age, it seems to have an incredible command over color, light and dark, the abstract and the concrete, and the emotional resonance that their careful combination can conjure.</p>
<p><img src="https://thesephist.com/img/dalle-2-images.jpg" alt="A 4x3 grid of diverse AI-generated artworks: abstract shapes, cosmic scenes, portraits, still life, surrealism, and landscapes, showcasing DALL-E 2&rsquo;s versatility in color, composition, and subject matter."></p>
<p><em>I picked these twelve images out of a much larger batch I generated with DALL-E 2 automatically by combining some randomly generated subjects with one of a few pre-written styles suffixes like ‚Äúwatercolor on canvas.‚Äù</em></p>
<p>Notice the use of shadows behind the body in the first image and the impressionistic use of color in the third image in the first row. I also love the softness of the silhouette in the top right, and the cyclops figure that seems to emerge beyond the horizon in the second row. Even in the most abstract images in this grid, the choice of color and composition result in something I would personally find not at all out of place in a gallery. There is surprising variety, creativity, and depth to these images, especially considering most of the prompts are as simple as <code>giving form to metaphor, watercolor on canvas</code> or <code>a cozy bedroom, still life composition</code>.</p>
<p>When I try to create similar kinds of images with what I believe to be the state-of-the-art text-to-image system today, Midjourney v6, here‚Äôs what I get with similar prompts.</p>
<p><img src="https://thesephist.com/img/midjourney-images-batch-1.jpg" alt="A 4x4 grid of surreal yet highly detailed artworks: vivid abstract portraits, photorealistic hands with eyes, intricate cityscapes with cats, and lifelike sleeping figures, showcasing more intense colors, cohesive themes, and significantly higher detail than the previous collection."></p>
<p>These images are beautiful in their own right, and in their detail and realism they are impressive. I‚Äôm regularly stunned by the quality and realism in images generated by Midjourney. This isn&rsquo;t meant to be another ‚ÄúAI-generated images aren&rsquo;t artistic‚Äù post.</p>
<p>However, there <em>is</em> a very obvious difference in the styles of these two systems. After having generated a few hundred images from both systems, I find DALL-E 2 to be regularly:</p>
<ul>
<li>surprising and creative with its use of color and contrast;</li>
<li>usually focused on a single subject, with one or few things going on in the frame;</li>
<li>quite inventive in how it interprets ambiguous or open-ended prompts, often bringing in varied appropriate subjects on its own.</li>
</ul>
<p>By contrast, Midjourney‚Äôs images are biased towards:</p>
<ul>
<li>a lot of detail all the time, almost as if the system really wants to put every single pixel to use;</li>
<li>pretty uniform, flat use of color compared to DALL-E 2‚Äôs;</li>
<li>literal interpretations of the prompt, with a tendency to have the image always be ‚Äúabout‚Äù something concrete with little room for ambiguity.</li>
</ul>
<p>Though Midjourney v6 is the most capable system like this in my experience, I encounter these same stylistic biases when using any modern model from the last couple of years, like Stable Diffusion XL and its derivatives, Google‚Äôs Imagen models, or even the current version of DALL-E (DALL-E 3). This is a shame, because I really like the variety and creativity of outputs from DALL-E 2, and it seems no modern systems are capable of reproducing similar results.</p>
<p>I&rsquo;ve also done some head-to-head comparisons, including giving Midjourney examples of images from which it could transfer the style. Though Midjourney v6 successfully copies the original image‚Äôs style, it still has the hallmark richness in detail, as well as a clear tendency towards concrete subjects like realistic human silhouettes:</p>
<p><img src="https://thesephist.com/img/dalle-2-midjourney-comparison.png" alt="Side-by-side comparison of two abstract watercolor-style images: DALL-E 2 on left with simpler, isolated hand shapes; Midjourney v6 on right with more complex, blended forms and richer color palette and more realistic human forms."></p>
<p>Though none of this is a scientifically rigorous study, I&rsquo;ve heard similar sentiments from other users of these systems, and observed similar ‚Äúun-creative‚Äù behavior from modern language models like ChatGPT. In particular, I found <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05587">this study of distribution of outputs before and after preference tuning on Llama 2</a> models interesting, because I think they successfully quantify the bland ‚ÄúChatGPT voice‚Äù and show some concrete ways that reinforcement learning has produced <a href="https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/t9svvNPNmFf5Qa3TA/mysteries-of-mode-collapse">accidental</a> <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attractor">attractors</a> in the model‚Äôs output space.</p>
<p>Why does this happen?</p>
<p>There are a few major differences between DALL-E 2 and other systems that we could hypothetically point to:</p>
<ul>
<li>They‚Äôre trained on very different datasets.</li>
<li>DALL-E 2 never underwent any preference tuning, where the other models have.</li>
<li>DALL-E 2 is the only pixel-space image diffusion model still in wide use. All other models perform diffusion in a compressed latent space, which may hamper things like the diversity of color palette.</li>
</ul>
<p>After thinking about it a bit and playing with some open source models, I think there are two big things going on here.</p>
<p>The first is that <strong>humans simply prefer brighter, more colorful, more detailed images when asked to pick a ‚Äúbetter‚Äù visual</strong> in a side-by-side comparison, even though they would not necessarily prefer a world in which every single image was so hyper-detailed and hyper-colorful. So when models are tuned to human preferences, they naturally produce these hyper-detailed, hyper-colorful sugar-pop images.</p>
<p>The second is that when a model is trained using a method with feedback loops like reinforcement learning, it tends towards ‚Äúattractors‚Äù, or preferred modes in the output space, and stops being an accurate model of reality in which every concept is proportionately represented in its output space. <strong>Preference tuning tunes models away from being accurate reflections of reality</strong> into being greedy reward-seekers happy to output a boring response if it expects the boring output to be rated highly.</p>
<p>Let&rsquo;s investigate these ideas in more detail.</p>
<h2 id="1-are-we-comparing-outputs-or-comparing-worlds">1. Are we comparing outputs, or comparing worlds?</h2>
<p>If you&rsquo;ve ever walked into an electronics store and looked at a wall full of TVs or listened to headphones on display, you‚Äôll notice they‚Äôre all tuned to the brightest, loudest, most vibrant settings. Sometimes, the colors are so vibrant they make pictures look a bit unrealistic, with perfectly turquoise oceans and perfectly tan skin.</p>
<p>In general, when asked to compare images or music, people with untrained eyes and ears will pick the brightest images and the loudest music. Bright images create the illusion of vibrant colors and greater detail, and make other images that are less bright seem dull. Loud music has a similar effect, giving rise to a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudness_war">‚Äúloudness war‚Äù</a> on public radio where tracks compete to catch listeners&rsquo; attention by being louder than other tracks before or after it.</p>
<p>Now, we are also in a loudness war of synthetic media.</p>
<p>Another way to think about this phenomenon is as a failure to align <em>what we are asking human labelers to compare</em> with <em>what we actually want to compare</em>.</p>
<p>When we build a preference dataset, what we should actually be asking is, ‚ÄúIs a world with a model trained on this dataset preferable to a world with a model trained on that dataset?‚Äù Of course, this is an intractable question to ask, because doing so would require somehow collecting human labels on every possible arrangement of a training dataset, leading to a combinatorial explosion of options. Instead, we approximate this by collecting human preference signals on each individual data point. But there‚Äôs a mismatch: just because humans prefer a more detailed image in one instance doesn&rsquo;t mean that we‚Äôd prefer a world where every single image was maximally detailed.</p>
<h2 id="2-building-attractors-out-of-world-models">2. Building attractors out of world models</h2>
<p>Preference tuning methods like RLHF and DPO are fundamentally different from the kind of supervised training that goes on during model pretraining or a ‚Äúbasic‚Äù fine-tuning run with labelled data, because methods like RL and DPO involve feeding the model‚Äôs output back into itself, creating a feedback loop.</p>
<p>Whenever there are feedback loops in a system, we can study its <em>dynamics</em> ‚Äî¬†over time, as we iterate towards infinity, does the system settle into some state of stability? Does it settle into a loop? Does it diverge, accelerating towards some limit?</p>
<p>In the case of systems like ChatGPT and Midjourney, these models appear to converge under feedback loops into a few <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attractor">attractors</a>, parts of the output space that the model has deemed reliably preferred, ‚Äúsafe‚Äù options. One attractor, for example, is a hyper-realistic detailed style of illustration. Another seems to be a fondness for geometric lines and transhumanist imagery when asked to generate anything abstract and vaguely positive.</p>
<p>I think recognizing this difference between base models and feedback-tuned models is important, because this kind of a preference tuning step changes what the model is doing at a fundamental level. A pretrained base model is an <strong>epistemically calibrated world model</strong>. It‚Äôs <strong>epistemically calibrated</strong>, meaning its output probabilities exactly mirror frequency of concepts and styles present in its training dataset. If 2% of all photos of waterfalls also have rainbows, exactly 2% of photos of waterfalls the model generates will have rainbows. It‚Äôs also a <strong>world model</strong>, in the sense that what results from pretraining is a probabilistic model of observations of the world (its training dataset). Anything we can find in the training dataset, we can also expect to find in the model‚Äôs output space.</p>
<p>Once we subject the model to preference tuning, however, the model transforms into something very different, a function that greedily and cleverly finds a way to interpret every input into a version of the request that includes elements it knows is most likely to result in a positive rating from a reviewer. Within the constraints of a given input, a model that‚Äôs undergone RLHF is no longer an accurate world model, but a function whose sole job is to find a way to render a version of the output that‚Äôs super detailed, very colorful, extremely polite, or whatever else the model has learned will please the recipient of its output. These reliably-rewarded concepts become attractors in the model‚Äôs output space. See also the apocryphal story about OpenAI‚Äôs model optimized for positive outputs, resulting in <a href="https://www.alignmentforum.org/posts/t9svvNPNmFf5Qa3TA/mysteries-of-mode-collapse#Inescapable_wedding_parties">inescapable wedding parties</a>.</p>
<p>Today&rsquo;s most effective tools for producing useful, obedient models irreversibly take away something quite valuable that base models have by construction: its epistemic calibration to the world it was trained on.</p>
<h2 id="interpretable-models-enable-useful-ai-without-mode-collapse">Interpretable models enable useful AI without mode collapse</h2>
<p>Though I find any individual output from ChatGPT or Midjourney useful and sometimes even beautiful, I can‚Äôt really say the same about the possibility space of outputs from these models at large. In tuning these models to our pointwise preferences, it feels like we lost the variety and creativity that enable these models to yield surprising and tasteful outputs.</p>
<p>Maybe there‚Äôs a way to build useful AI systems without the downsides of mode collapse.</p>
<p>Preference tuning is necessary today because of the way we currently interact with these AI systems, as black boxes which take human input and produce some output. To bend these black boxes to our will, we must reprogram their internals to want to yield output we prefer.</p>
<p>But there‚Äôs another growing paradigm for interacting with AI systems, one where we <a href="https://thesephist.com/posts/prism">directly manipulate concepts within a model‚Äôs internal feature space</a> to elicit outputs we desire. Using these methods, we no longer have to subject the model to a damaging preference tuning process. We can search the model‚Äôs concept space directly for the kinds of outputs we desire and sample them directly from a base model. Want a sonnet about the future of quantum computing that‚Äôs written from the perspective of a cat? Locate those concepts within the model, activate them mechanistically, and sample the model outputs. No instructions necessary.</p>
<p>Mechanistic steering like this is still early in research, and for now we have to make do with simpler tasks like changing the topic and writing style of short sentences. But I find this approach very promising because it could give us a way to make pretrained models useful without turning them into overeager human-pleasers that fall towards an attractor at the first chance they get.</p>
<p>Furthermore, sampling directly from a model‚Äôs concept space allows us to rigorously quantify qualities like <em>diversity of output</em> that we can‚Äôt control well in currently deployed models. Want variety in your outputs? Simply expand the radius around which you‚Äôre searching in the model‚Äôs latent space.</p>
<p>This world ‚Äî directly interacting with epistemically calibrated models ‚Äî¬†isn&rsquo;t incompatible with Midjourney-style hyper-realistic hyper-detailed images either. Perhaps when we have in our hands a well-understood, capable model of the world‚Äôs images we‚Äôll find not only all the abstract images from DALL-E 2 and all the intricate illustrations from Midjourney, but an uncountable number of styles and preferences in between, as many as we have time to enumerate as we explore its vast space of knowledge.</p> 

<https://thesephist.com/posts/epistemic-calibration/>
