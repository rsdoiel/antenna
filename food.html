MAY DAY IN IRELAND: THE FOODS OF BEALTAINE

(date: 2026-05-05)

Long before modern calendars marked the start of summer, the Irish marked it in a very different way. Bealtaine, celebrated on May 1st, was one of the most important festivals in the ancient Gaelic year—a turning point between the darkness of winter and the brightness of the growing season. It was a time of fire, protection, renewal, and quiet celebration. While Bealtaine is often remembered for its rituals—bonfires, May bushes, and protective customs—it was also a moment that touched the table. The foods associated with May Day in Ireland were simple, seasonal, and deeply symbolic. They reflected the land’s awakening, the return of milk and growth, and the hope for abundance in the months ahead. Unlike later feast days, Bealtaine was not about lavish meals. It was about marking change—using what was available, and preparing for what was to come. It was a festival rooted in agriculture, signalling the movement of livestock to summer pastures and the renewal of the land. Fire played a central role—bonfires were lit for protection, and cattle were driven between them to safeguard their health. Food, though less dramatic than the fires, was part of this same rhythm. It was tied to protection, gratitude, and the hope for a good season. The table reflected not abundance, but transition—a movement from scarcity toward growth. A Seasonal Turning Point In early May, Ireland sat between two worlds. Winter stores were running low, but summer crops were not yet fully ready. This meant that Bealtaine foods were often simple, relying on staples that had lasted through the colder months, alongside the first signs of new growth. This in-between season shaped everything. Meals were not extravagant, but they carried meaning. Even small additions—fresh milk, early greens, or newly churned butter—felt significant. The Return of Milk and Dairy One of the most important food elements of Bealtaine was dairy. As cows returned to richer pasture, milk production increased, making fresh milk, butter, and buttermilk more available after the leaner months. This made dairy a symbol of renewal and prosperity. Fresh butter, in particular, held a special place—it represented both nourishment and the success of the coming season. In some traditions, butter was even associated with protection, carefully guarded against folklore fears of it being “taken” or spoiled through superstition. Simple foods like bread and butter, or porridge enriched with fresh milk, became quietly celebratory in this context. Oats, Bread, and Everyday Staples Oats remained a cornerstone of the Irish diet at Bealtaine, just as they had been throughout the year. Porridge, oatcakes, and simple breads provided reliable nourishment, especially when other foods were limited. On May Day, these staples might be prepared with a little more care or served with the season’s first dairy. A warm oatcake with fresh butter could mark the day just as meaningfully as any feast. Bread—whether griddle bread or early forms of soda bread—also played a role. It was practical, filling, and easily shared, making it a natural centrepiece for small gatherings. Wild Greens and Early Harvest Foods As the land began to wake, wild greens started to appear—nettles, sorrel, and other early plants that could be gathered and added to meals. These were among the first fresh foods after winter, bringing both nutrition and a sense of renewal. Nettle soup, in particular, has long been associated with spring in Ireland. Though not exclusive to Bealtaine, it reflects the same seasonal shift: a return to fresh, living ingredients after months of stored food. These early greens weren’t just practical—they symbolized health, vitality, and connection to the land. Food and Protection Rituals Bealtaine was deeply tied to protection, and food often played a role in these customs. Some traditions involved leaving small portions of food outside—offerings meant to ensure good fortune or to ward off harm. Milk and butter, because of their importance, were sometimes included in these rituals. The idea was not just to nourish the body, but to safeguard the household and livestock for the months ahead. Even within the home, food could carry symbolic weight. The act of preparing and sharing a meal on May Day was part of a wider effort to begin the season well. Sharing Food and Community Traditions Like many Irish traditions, Bealtaine was not experienced in isolation. It was a community event, even if the food itself was simple. Neighbours gathered, fires were shared, and small acts of hospitality took on greater meaning. Food might be exchanged, shared, or prepared together. A loaf of bread, a pot of porridge, or a simple meal eaten outdoors could mark the occasion. These moments reinforced community ties and created a shared sense of hope for the season ahead. Bealtaine Foods Today Today, Bealtaine is often celebrated in a more symbolic or cultural way, but its food traditions still offer inspiration. Modern interpretations might include seasonal Irish dishes that reflect the same themes: freshness, simplicity, and connection to the land. A contemporary Bealtaine table might feature fresh dairy, rustic bread, spring greens, and light, nourishing dishes. It doesn’t need to be elaborate to be meaningful—just thoughtful and seasonal. In rediscovering these traditions, we reconnect with a slower, more grounded approach to food—one that values the time of year, the quality of ingredients, and the act of sharing a meal. They reflect a moment when the land begins to give again, when the days grow longer, and when hope quietly returns to the table. The firm, meaty texture of monkfish pairs beautifully with the crisp topping, creating a flavorful and elegant meal perfect for any occasion. Preparation Time: 15 minutesCooking Time: 25 minutesServing Size: Serves 4 Ingredients 4 monkfish fillets 2 tbsp olive oil Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste 1 cup fresh breadcrumbs 2 tbsp fresh parsley, finely chopped 1 tbsp fresh thyme leaves 1 clove garlic, minced Zest of 1 lemon 2 tbsp melted butter Lemon wedges, for serving Instructions Preheat oven: Preheat to 400°F (200°C). Prepare fish: Place monkfish fillets in a lightly oiled baking dish. Season with salt and pepper. Make herb crust: In a bowl, combine breadcrumbs, parsley, thyme, garlic, lemon zest, and melted butter. Mix until evenly coated. Top fish: Press the herb mixture onto the top of each fillet. Bake: Bake for 20–25 minutes until the fish is cooked through and the crust is golden and crisp. Serve: Serve hot with lemon wedges. MAYO BOXTY DUMPLING SOUP

(date: 2026-05-05)

Description: A comforting Irish soup inspired by County Mayo, this dish features soft, pillowy boxty dumplings simmered in a rich, savory broth with vegetables and herbs. Hearty and warming, it blends the tradition of potato-based cooking with a nourishing farmhouse-style soup. Preparation Time: 20 minutesCooking Time: 45 minutesServing Size: Serves 4–6 Ingredients 1 tbsp butter or oil 1 onion, chopped 2 carrots, sliced 2 celery stalks, chopped 2 cloves garlic, minced 6 cups chicken or vegetable stock 1 tsp fresh thyme (or 1/2 tsp dried) Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste 2 tbsp fresh parsley, chopped For the Boxty Dumplings 1 cup grated raw potato (squeezed dry) 1 cup mashed potato 1/2 cup all-purpose flour 1/2 tsp baking powder 1/2 tsp salt 1 egg 2–3 tbsp milk Instructions Start the soup base: Heat butter or oil in a large pot. Add onion, carrots, and celery and cook for 5–7 minutes until softened. Add garlic and stock: Stir in garlic, then pour in the stock. Add thyme, salt, and pepper. Bring to a gentle simmer. Make dumpling dough: In a bowl, combine grated potato, mashed potato, flour, baking powder, salt, egg, and milk. Mix to form a soft dough. Add dumplings: Drop spoonfuls of the dough into the simmering soup. Cook: Cover and simmer gently for 15–20 minutes until dumplings are cooked through and fluffy. Finish: Taste and adjust seasoning. Stir in fresh parsley before serving. Serving Suggestion Serve hot with crusty Irish bread for a hearty, traditional meal perfect for cold days.

IRISH DESSERTS THAT DESERVE A COMEBACK

(date: 2026-04-22, updated: 2026-05-05)

Irish desserts have never been about excess. They were shaped by simple ingredients, seasonal rhythms, and the quiet creativity of home kitchens. For generations, sweet dishes in Ireland were made not to impress, but to comfort—to bring warmth after a long day, or to mark a small celebration with whatever was on hand. Yet many of these traditional desserts have slowly faded from everyday life. Modern baking trends, convenience foods, and global influences have pushed some of Ireland’s most humble and meaningful sweet dishes into the background. But in recent years, there’s been a growing appreciation for these forgotten treats—recipes that deserve not just to be remembered, but revived. Here are some Irish desserts that once held pride of place on the table—and are ready for a well-earned comeback. Table of Contents Gur Cake Irish Apple Cake Bread and Butter Pudding Sweet Boxty Carrageen Moss Pudding Porter Cake Traditional Milk Puddings Gur Cake Few desserts are as deeply tied to Dublin’s working-class history as gur cake. Made from leftover bread or cake crumbs mixed with dried fruit and spices, then sandwiched between layers of pastry, it was a bakery staple that turned scraps into something surprisingly rich and satisfying. Gur cake wasn’t fancy—it was practical. But that practicality is exactly what makes it so appealing today. In a world focused on reducing waste and embracing sustainability, this dessert feels more relevant than ever. With its dense texture and spiced sweetness, it deserves a place in modern kitchens once again. Irish Apple Cake Before elaborate layer cakes became popular, the Irish apple cake was the go-to homemade dessert. Made with tart apples, a simple batter, and often finished with a dusting of sugar or a light custard, it was the kind of bake that filled the house with warmth. What sets Irish apple cake apart is its balance—it’s not overly sweet, allowing the natural flavour of the apples to shine. While it still appears in some homes and cafés, it has been overshadowed by richer desserts. A return to this simple, honest bake would be a return to what Irish baking does best. Bread and Butter Pudding Once a cornerstone of Irish home cooking, bread and butter pudding made use of stale bread layered with butter, sugar, and dried fruit, then baked in a custard mixture until golden and soft. It was economical, filling, and endlessly adaptable. Over time, it became associated with older generations and was gradually replaced by more modern desserts. But its comforting texture and deep flavour make it a perfect candidate for revival—especially as people rediscover the value of simple, ingredient-driven cooking. Sweet Boxty Boxty is best known as a savoury potato dish, but in some regions, it also appeared in sweeter forms. Sweet boxty might include sugar, butter, or even fruit, turning a staple ingredient into a dessert. This version has largely disappeared from modern menus, but it offers something unique: a dessert rooted in Ireland’s most iconic ingredient—the potato. In an era where chefs are exploring new uses for traditional foods, sweet boxty could easily find its way back into contemporary Irish cuisine. Carrageen Moss Pudding Carrageen moss pudding is perhaps one of the most uniquely Irish desserts on this list. Made using a type of seaweed found along Ireland’s coast, it creates a naturally set, delicate pudding when boiled with milk and sweetened. Flavoured with vanilla, sugar, or even a touch of whiskey, it was once a common treat in coastal areas. Today, it is rarely seen outside of traditional food circles. Yet with the growing interest in natural ingredients and sea vegetables, carrageen moss pudding feels perfectly suited for a modern comeback. Porter Cake Rich, dark, and full of character, porter cake is a fruit cake made with stout beer, giving it a deep flavour and moist texture. It was often baked for special occasions but didn’t require elaborate decoration—its taste spoke for itself. While fruit cakes have fallen out of fashion in many places, porter cake stands apart. Its connection to Irish brewing and its bold flavour make it a natural fit for revival, especially alongside the continued popularity of Irish stout. Traditional Milk Puddings Milk-based puddings—simple mixtures of milk, sugar, and thickening agents—were once common in Irish households. They were easy to prepare, gentle in flavour, and often served warm or chilled depending on the season. These desserts reflected the importance of dairy in Ireland’s food culture. Today, they are often overlooked in favour of more complex sweets, but their simplicity is exactly what makes them appealing. In a fast-paced world, there is something comforting about a dessert that requires so little yet delivers so much. Why These Desserts Matter Today The revival of traditional Irish desserts is about more than nostalgia. It’s about reconnecting with a way of cooking that values resourcefulness, seasonality, and authenticity. These dishes remind us that great food doesn’t need to be complicated—it needs to be thoughtful. As interest in Irish food continues to grow, both in Ireland and abroad, there is an opportunity to bring these forgotten desserts back into everyday life. Whether through home baking, cafés, or modern reinterpretations, they offer a taste of something genuine. Closing: A Sweet Part of Irish Heritage Irish desserts tell a story—of families, of seasons, and of making the most of what was available. While trends may come and go, these dishes have stood the test of time for a reason. They are simple, satisfying, and deeply rooted in Irish life. Bringing them back isn’t about looking backward—it’s about carrying forward a tradition that still has something to offer. And sometimes, the sweetest discoveries are the ones that were there all along.

CONNEMARA LAMB AND SEAWEED HOTPOT

(date: 2026-04-22, updated: 2026-05-05)

Description: A rustic and deeply traditional dish inspired by Ireland’s west coast, this Connemara lamb hotpot combines tender lamb, hearty root vegetables, and mineral-rich seaweed. Slow-cooked until rich and comforting, it reflects the rugged flavors of the Atlantic shoreline.
