IRISH CRAFT SODA MAKERS: THE SPARKLING SIDE OF THE DRINKS SCENE

(date: 2025-12-15)

Table of Contents Introduction: Ireland’s Sparkling Revival The Roots of Irish Soft Drinks Ireland’s Most Influential Craft Soda Makers Flavours Inspired by the Irish Landscape Where to Taste Irish Craft Sodas Today Ireland’s Sparkling Revival Across Ireland, a quiet but exciting shift is taking place in the world of soft drinks. Where once the shelves were dominated by familiar global brands, a new wave of Irish craft soda makers is bringing creativity, natural ingredients, and local flavour back to the fizzy drinks scene. These small-batch producers are redefining what a soda can be—less sugary, more botanical, and proudly rooted in Irish landscapes and traditions. This sparkling revival is driven by changing tastes. More people are seeking non-alcoholic options that still feel special, and Irish producers have responded with sodas infused with elderflower, rhubarb, lemon verbena, wild berries, sea salt, and botanicals gathered from hedgerows and gardens. Instead of artificial flavours or syrup-heavy formulas, these drinks celebrate real fruit, natural sweetness, and artisanal methods. But the movement isn’t just about flavour—it’s about identity. Craft soda makers often work closely with Irish growers, beekeepers, and foragers, turning local ingredients into refreshing drinks that tell a story. Whether enjoyed on their own or used as mixers for non-alcoholic cocktails, these sodas capture the brightness and creativity of modern Irish food culture. The Roots of Irish Soft Drinks Long before craft sodas became fashionable, Ireland had a distinctive soft drink culture shaped by mineral-rich springs, Victorian temperance movements, and rural home traditions. These early influences helped create a national fondness for sparkling beverages, many of which remain iconic today. In the 18th and 19th centuries, Ireland developed a reputation for its natural mineral wells. Places like Lisdoonvarna in County Clare and Lucan in County Dublin became known for their effervescent waters, believed to promote health and vitality. Local bottlers began capturing these naturally bubbly sources, laying the groundwork for Ireland’s soft drink trade. As bottling technology improved, small family-run companies across the country produced traditional lemonades, tonics, and sodas. These were often enjoyed at fairs, markets, and family gatherings. The famous “red lemonade”—a beloved Irish classic still found in shops today—became an iconic drink associated with childhood treats, pubs, and special occasions. Its exact origin remains a matter of folklore, but its popularity helped define Ireland’s soft drink identity. In rural homes, people also made their own natural sodas and cordials from elderflower, blackberries, rhubarb, and gooseberries. These homemade drinks often followed seasonal rhythms—elderflower in early summer, berries in late summer, and apples and herbs in autumn. Many of today’s craft soda makers draw directly from these traditional flavour profiles. By the mid-20th century, larger industrial brands dominated the market, and many small bottlers disappeared. But the memory of natural flavours, family-run soda works, and homemade cordials never faded. This nostalgia—and a renewed desire for authentic Irish ingredients—created the perfect backdrop for today’s craft soda revival. Ireland’s Most Influential Craft Soda Makers Across Ireland, a growing number of small-scale producers are transforming simple fizzy drinks into artisanal creations inspired by Irish landscapes, gardens, and traditional flavours. These craft soda makers are blending old-fashioned techniques with modern creativity, offering sodas that feel fresh, local, and genuinely unique. Here are some of the producers leading the sparkling renaissance. Wilde’s Irish Soda Co. Inspired by Ireland’s literary past and botanical heritage, Wilde’s creates elegant sodas infused with garden herbs, hedgerow botanicals, and real fruit. Their flavours—such as elderflower & gooseberry, lemon verbena, and blackberry & basil—reflect a commitment to natural ingredients and balanced sweetness. Each bottle feels like a taste of Irish summer, captured and carbonated. King of Kefir Based in Dublin, King of Kefir takes a different approach by offering naturally fermented probiotic sodas made with traditional water kefir grains. Their drinks are low-sugar, gently fizzy, and crafted with flavours such as ginger & lemon, raspberry, hibiscus, and hopped citrus. They appeal to those seeking gut-friendly alternatives to conventional soft drinks while still enjoying vibrant, refreshing flavours. Poacher’s Irish Mixers Though known primarily for their premium mixers, Poacher’s has become influential in Ireland’s craft soda scene through their use of local botanicals and Irish spring water. Their citrus sodas, herb-infused tonics, and spiced ginger sodas are widely used in alcohol-free cocktails and across Ireland’s bars, cafés, and hotels. Poacher’s embodies the movement toward clean, crisp, naturally flavoured sparkling drinks. Longueville House Apple Soda In County Cork, Longueville House—famous for its orchard and brandy production—creates a beautifully simple apple soda using estate-grown fruit. Lightly sparkling and naturally sweet, it highlights the terroir of Irish orchards. This soda pairs especially well with cheeses, roast pork, and desserts, making it popular in farm shops and artisan markets. Regional Micro-Producers All around Ireland, small family operations and local innovators are experimenting with craft soda recipes. In Galway and Mayo, producers are turning to wild berries, rhubarb, and botanicals. In Wicklow, many sodas draw inspiration from the county’s gardens and beekeeping culture. In Donegal and Sligo, entrepreneurs craft sea-inspired blends using Atlantic salt, citrus, and herbs. Though diverse in style and scale, these producers share a common goal: to make fizzy drinks that feel rooted in Ireland—its fields, orchards, hedgerows, and coastal breezes. A Coastal Pub-Style Snack Description: These Potato & Dillisk Fritters capture the flavours of Ireland’s coastal pubs. Preparation Time: 20 minutesCooking Time: 15 minutesServing Size: Makes 10–12 fritters Ingredients 500g (about 3 medium) floury potatoes, peeled and diced 2 tbsp dried dillisk (dulse), crumbled 1 spring onion, finely sliced 1 large egg 3–4 tbsp plain flour ½ tsp baking powder Salt and black pepper, to taste Neutral oil for shallow frying Optional: 1 tsp lemon zest, ½ tsp smoked paprika, chopped fresh parsley Instructions Step 1 – Cook the Potatoes Boil the diced potatoes in salted water until tender. Drain thoroughly and mash while hot, allowing steam to escape so the mixture stays dry. Step 2 – Add Dillisk and Seasonings Stir in the crumbled dillisk, spring onion, salt, pepper, and any optional ingredients such as lemon zest. Step 3 – Bind the Mixture Add the egg and mix well. Step 4 – Fry the Fritters Heat a shallow layer of oil in a frying pan over medium heat. Drop spoonfuls of the mixture into the pan and gently flatten. WILD BERRIES OF IRELAND: RECIPES AND TRADITIONS

(date: 2025-12-02)

Across Ireland’s hedgerows, mountain slopes, and woodland edges, wild berries have ripened for thousands of years—feeding families, flavouring feasts, and inspiring folklore. From the juicy blackberries of late summer to the sharp sloes that signal autumn’s arrival, these berries form one of Ireland’s oldest natural food sources. They are reminders of seasons, stories, and skills passed down through generations. Long before supermarkets or sugar arrived on the island, Irish people relied on nature’s rhythms. Berries offered sweetness in an otherwise simple diet and were prized for preserves, drinks, remedies, and celebration foods. Picking them was not just a chore—it was a treasured ritual, especially for children who carried home purple-stained hands after rambling through brambles. Today, as interest in foraging and traditional foodways returns, Ireland’s wild berries are being rediscovered. Modern chefs use them in desserts and sauces, while home cooks revive old jams, syrups, and cordials. For many, gathering berries is still a moment of connection—to the land, the past, and the flavours that make Ireland unique. Table of Contents 1. Introduction: Ireland’s Wild Larder 2. A Guide to Ireland’s Native Wild Berries 3. Foraging Traditions and Folklore 4. Old Irish Recipes With Wild Berries 5. Modern Irish Cooking With Wild Berries 6. Where to Forage in Ireland Today Ireland’s Wild Larder Wild berries are one of Ireland’s most accessible and rewarding natural foods. They require no planting and no tending—only a season’s patience and a keen eye. For centuries, they were some of the few sweet treats available to rural families. Blackberry picking became an annual ritual, bilberries were gathered on windswept hills, and elderberries found their way into winter remedies and deep red cordials. These berries played important roles beyond the kitchen. They marked the changing seasons, featured in festival customs such as Lughnasa and Michaelmas, and held a place in folk medicine. Some were thought to protect against ill luck; others were believed dangerous to pick after certain dates. Their stories reveal Ireland’s long, intimate relationship with the land. A Guide to Ireland’s Native Wild Berries Ireland’s landscape is rich with wild berries—tiny bursts of flavour that mark the passing of summer into autumn. Each berry carries its own history, habitat, and traditional uses, forming a natural pantry that Irish families have relied on for centuries. From mountain heaths to hedgerows, here are the berries that have shaped Ireland’s seasonal food culture. Blackberries (Brambles) Perhaps the most iconic of Ireland’s wild berries, blackberries ripen from late August to October. Found in hedgerows, laneways, and woodland edges, they were once every child’s first foraging experience. Sweet, juicy, and intensely flavoured, they were used for pies, jams, stewed desserts, and simple eating straight from the bramble. Their abundance made them a vital seasonal food for rural families. Bilberries (Fraocháns) Known as fraocháns in Irish, these small dark-blue berries grow on low shrubs in upland areas, especially in Mayo, Donegal, and Wicklow. Traditionally gathered during Lughnasa celebrations, they were eaten fresh, baked into tarts, or mixed with cream. Their earthy, slightly tart flavour remains cherished in regions where bilberry traditions survive. Elderberries Elder trees offer two gifts: fragrant blossoms in early summer and rich purple berries in autumn. Elderberries were prized for syrups, tonics, and winter remedies due to their reputed immune-boosting properties. Cooked into jams or cordials, they brought deep colour and flavour to the Irish pantry. Raw elderberries should not be eaten, but when cooked they are wonderfully aromatic. Sloe Berries These dark, sharp berries grow on blackthorn bushes and are notoriously sour when raw. But after the first frost—or a spell in the freezer—they transform. Traditionally used to make sloe gin and hedgerow liqueurs, sloes steep beautifully with sugar and spirit, producing a warming drink cherished through the winter months. Hawberries Bright red hawthorn berries decorate hedgerows in early autumn. Though not naturally sweet, they were historically boiled into jellies or added to stews for colour and nutrition. In folk medicine, hawthorn was linked to heart health, and its berries, leaves, and blossoms were used in herbal remedies. Rowan Berries Once believed to protect against evil and misfortune, rowan berries are bright, bitter, and tart. They were cooked into sharp jellies—often served with game or lamb—and were valued for their high vitamin content. The rowan tree itself held deep symbolic meaning in Irish folklore.
